## The body can synthesize only some amino acids
Of the 20 amino acids in the body, 9 of them are **essential**.  This just means that our body cannot produce enough/any of it so we need to eat it.  Without enough we would not be able to make sufficient proteins/other nitrogen based compounds.

**Nonessential** amino acids are just as important but our body can synthesize enough of them on its own, so we don't need to eat them.  They are made by transferring the amine group from other amino acids to a different acid group and side chain.  This is called **transamination**.  The acid groups and side chains can be donated by amino acids, or they could be made from breaking down the products of carbs and fats.  Thus by combining parts of different amino acids, we can make the necessary nonessential amino acids ourselves.

Sometimes a nonessential guy can become essential.  This is called a **conditionally essential amino acid**.  PKU is a disease where you can't metabolize phenylalanine (essential), which is used to produce tyrosine (non-essential).  This person would need to metabolize tyrosine in their diet itself since they don't have an phenylalanine to produce it from.

## Amino acids bond to form peptides
When two amino acids join together, the amine group of one binds to the acid group of the other one in a special chemical bond called a **peptide bond**.  A molecule of water is released as a by-product of this dehydration synthesis reaction.

- Two amino acids joined together make a **dipeptide**
- Three make a **tripeptide**
- Four to nine make a **oligopeptide**
- 10+ make a **polypeptide**

## Genes regulate amino acid binding
Every one is unique since we inherited a specific genetic code that combines the code from each of our parents.  Everyones genetic code makes minor differences in amino acid sequences, which lead to differences in our bodies individual proteins.  These result in the unique physical characteristics everyone has.

### Structure of genes
A gene is a segment of **deoxyribonucleic acid** ([[Nucleic acids|DNA]]) that is used as a guide for how to synthesize a particular protein.  All cells have the full set of someone's genes, but some cells only use some of your genes.  **Gene expression** is the process by which cells use genes to make proteins.

The building blocks of DNA are **nucleotides**, which are molecules made up of a "backbone" (phosphate group and a pentose sugar), where one of four nitrogenous bases are attached (adenine, guanine, cytosine, or thymine).  

Within DNA molecules, these nucleotides occur in two long parallel chains coiled into the shape of a double helix.  They vary only in their nitrogenous bases and the crazy amount of variance DNA has comes from the precise sequencing of the nucleotides.

Chains of nucleotides are held together by hydrogen bonds that link the nitrogenous bases.  Each base can only bond to its **complementary** base.  A to T, G to C.  The complementary nature of bases guides the transfer of the genetic instructions from the DNA to the protein.

### Transcription and translation
Proteins are made at [[Organelles|ribosomes]], but DNA never leaves the nucleus.  So for gene expression to happen, the DNA needs to replicate itself, where the copy is carried to the cytoplasm and to the ribosome.  This is how one DNA can give the instructions to build every protein in the body.

Cells use a special molecule to copy (**transcribe**) the information.  This molecule is **messenger RNA** (mRNA).  RNA is a single strand of nucleotides rather than two and its four nitrogenous bases are A, G, C, and U.  It has the pentose sugar ribose instead of deoxyribose.  During transcription, mRNA copies the DNA's base sequencing to its own base sequencing and detaches from the DNA then leaves the nucleus, carrying its message to the ribosomes.

Once it gets to the ribosomes **translation** occurs.  The language of mRNA sequences gets turned into the language of amino acid sequences.  At the ribosome, mRNA binds with RNA and its nucleotide sequences get distributed like orders to molecules of transfer RNA (tRNA).  Now the tRNA roam around the cytoplasm until they find the specific amino acid that matches their order, then they transfer their amino acid to the ribosome which assembles them all into proteins. 

Once all amino acids are loaded onto the ribosome, tRNA works to move everything into its proper position, once complete, it the new protein gets released from the ribosome to be either further modified by the cell or sent off.

The proper sequencing of amino acids determines the shape and function of a protein.  Genetic abnormalities come from when someone inherits genes with errors in proper nucleotide sequencing which make faulty proteins.  Like PKL, sickle cell disease, and cystic fibrosis.

Even though every cell has all our DNA in it, each one only uses some of them and makes only certain types of proteins.  Like only the beta cells of the pancreas express the insulin gene to produce insulin.
