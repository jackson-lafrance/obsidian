## Why we feel the urge to eat
Two mechanisms prompt us to seek food:

**Hunger** is the physiologic drive for food that occurs when the body realizes that we need to eat.  This drive is **nonspecific**, when you're hungry any different food can satisfy you.  If you've just eaten you won't be hungry for dessert.  

**Appetite** is the psychological desire to eat specific foods, which is brought about by environmental queues, like the sight of cake or smell of coffee.  It stimulates our senses and prompts pleasant emotions and memories.

People can experience appetite even without being hungry.  This is what makes you still want dessert even after you've eaten a full meal.  It's also possible to have the opposite, which is called **anorexia**.

## The hypothalamus prompts hunger
Since hunger is a physiologic stimulus that drives us to find and eat food, we usually feel it as an unpleasant sensation.  The main organ that produces this is the brain, not our stomachs.  The region of brain tissue responsible for this is called the hypothalamus.  It's found right above the pituitary gland and brain stem region and it's important for regulating lots of involuntary activity.

The hypothalamus has a cluster of nerve cells known as the feeding centre.  Stimulation of the feeding centre makes us feel hungry.  On the other hand, the satiety centre inhibits the feeding centre cells.  These work together to regulate food intake by integrating signals from three main sources called the [[Hunger signals and hormones]]:

-  Nerve cells in the GI system
- Chemical messenger molecules called hormones
- The amount and type of food we eat

## Environmental cues prompt appetite
While hunger is prompted by internal signals, appetite gets triggered by our environment.  The most significant factors are sensory data, social and cultural cues, and learning.

### The role of sensory data
The sight, smell, test, texture, and sound of foods stimulate our sensory receptors, which send signals to the brain.  Pretty foods appeal to our sense of sight, so food producers spend millions to get their food packaged in a specific way. 

Aromas of food are also powerful stimulants.  A lot of our ability to taste food comes from the smell actually.  Thats why foods aren't as appealing when we have a stuffy nose.  Texture/mouth feel is also important, because it stimulates nerve endings on our tongues.  Even hearing the fizz of a cola or the crunch of chips can stimulate appetite.

### The role of social and cultural cues
The brain associates certain events like birthday parties, with specific foods and this can stimulate our appetite.  Some people feel an increased appetite depending on who they are with.  Its the same thing for certain activities, experiences, or emotions (stress eating).  Other people seek food as a "reward".

### The role of learning
Food preferences are heavily influenced by the family, community, religion, and/or culture where you are raised.  This is learned food preference.  Like my chicken pot pie thing, people can learn to dislike foods they once enjoyed.
