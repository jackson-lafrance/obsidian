## Carbohydrate digestion
Carbohydrate digestion starts in the mouth when food mixes with saliva.  Saliva has an enzyme called **salivary amylase**.  Amyl means starch so this breaks down bread and other starches.  Disaccharides are not digested in the mouth.  As the bolus leaves the mouth and enters the stomach all carbs stop being digested.  Thats because stomach acid stops salivary amylase.

The majority of carb digestion is in the **small intestine**.  When the food enters, **pancreatic amylase** gets shot into the small intestine.  It digests the rest of the starch into maltose.  Extra enzymes in the microvilli of the [[Nutrient absorption and transport|enterocytes]] break down disaccharides into monosaccharides: **maltase**, **sucrase**, and **lactase**.

## Absorption
Once the carbs are fully digested, all monosaccharides are absorbed across the enterocytes and enter the bloodstream:
- **Glucose** and **galactose** with **active transport** using a carrier protein saturated with sodium.  This needs ATP energy
- **Fructose** is absorbed with **facilitated diffusion** and doesn't need any energy.  Fructose takes longer.  This is why it leads to diarrhea

## The liver converts non-glucose into glucose
Once they enter the bloodstream they go straight to the liver where everyone gets turned into glucose.  If this glucose is needed right away, it gets released into the bloodstream so it can travel to cells and provide energy.  Otherwise it gets stored as **glycogen**.

- Liver and muscles can **anabolic** make glycogen or **catabolic** revert to glucose
- Carb loading can increase muscle storage of glycogen
- Excess glucose is stored as fat in adipose tissue

## Fiber
Fiber gets excreted by the large intestine.  When its in the large intestine/colon, it gets fermented by bacteria which produces hydrogen, methane, and sulfur and some short chain fatty acids.  The cells in the large intestine use these for energy.
