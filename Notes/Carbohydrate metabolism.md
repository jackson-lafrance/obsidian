## How energy is extracted from carbs
Most carbohydrates that we eat are [[Carbohydrate digestion|digested and absorbed]] as glucose, which is then transported to the liver where it has a few fates:
- It can be phosphorylated and stored as [[Complex carbohydrates|glycogen]] in the liver
- It can be phosphorylated and used for energy or to make other compounds
- It can be released into circulation for other cells to use or stored as muscle tissue
- It can be converted to fat when consumed in excess and stored in adipose tissue

What happens to fructose and galactose though, the other [[Simple carbohydrates|monosaccharides]] is different.  They can be converted to glucose, or channeled into the glycolysis pathway for energy production.

The oxidation of glucose for the production of energy progresses through three stages:
1. Glycolysis
2. Tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle (Krebs cycle)
3. The electron transport chain

## In glycolysis, glucose is broken down into pyruvate
Glycolysis happens in the cytosol and doesn't need oxygen.  This lets any cell do it at any time (even during high intensity exercise).  The first step is the [[Metabolic reactions|phosphorylation]] of glucose, which makes glucose 6-phosphate and ADP.  The ATP that fuels this is stored in the cell that it happens in.  Then a bunch of enzyme reactions make pyruvate.  We need 2 ATP but it makes 4 ATP so it is a gain of 2 ATP.

Glycolysis is oxidative, since two hydrogen atoms are released which connect with NAD to make NADH.

If the pyruvate molecules are going to be used for the production of energy there are a few extra steps they have to go through depending on whether they are in an aerobic or anaerobic environment.  If energy isn't needed right away we can remake glucose and store it.

## In the absence of oxygen, pyruvate becomes lactate
When we are in an anaerobic environment, the pyruvate produced by glycolysis is turned into lactate where they take back the hydrogens and the guys who had it turn back into NAD.  This conversion to lactate happens in cells without [[Mitochondria and ATP|mitochondria]] or muscles during high intensity exercise.  It is not efficient since the conversion from pyruvate to lactate doesn't make any ATP so only the first reaction gives some.  But it lets NAD regenerate so glycolysis can continue.

This is why we can't sustain high intensity exercise forever since its inefficient.  After exercise lactate diffuses from muscle cells into the blood where it goes to the liver to be remade into pyruvate so it can turn back into glucose.

## In the presence of oxygen, pyruvate becomes acetyl CoA
When there is lots of oxygen pyruvate becomes a two carbon compound called **acetyl CoA**.  This happens in the mitochondria and it generates NADH + H from NAD+.  And it also expels a carbon which gets emitted as CO2 as a waste product.  We can't go back to glucose though so once it gets made it can only make ATP or turn into [[Fat metabolism|fatty acids]].  This step is important since it links glycolysis to the Krebs cycle.  

Once turned into acetyl CoA the Krebs cycle can start in the mitochondria which is a loop of eight metabolic reactions.  Other stuff is needed for it to keep functioning so its not really a cycle.  GTP is produced by the Krebs cycle which is equivalent to one ATP and so are some coenzymes, which help transition to stage three of the oxidation of glucose.  In the first two steps one molecule of glucose produces, 6 CO2, 2 ATP, 2 GTP, and 10 coenzymes as sources of H and e-.

## Oxidative phosphorylation captures energy as ATP
The final stage occurs in the electron transport chain and happens in the inner membrane of the mitochondria.  It is a bunch of reactions driven by enzymes where proteins called electron carriers accept and donate electrons.  As electrons are passed down the line ATP is released and NADH and FADH2 are oxidized and donated to O2 which becomes H2O.  This is the end of the glucose oxidation and it ends with the production of CO2, H2O and ATP.  ~30 net ATP can be made by one glucose.
