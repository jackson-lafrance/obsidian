## Complex carbs
These carbs contain long chains of glucose molecules.  Any carbohydrate with 3 or more monosaccharides are considered complex carbohydrates.

### Oligosaccharides
These are carbs with 3 -> 10 monosaccharides (oligo means few).  Two of the most common of these are **raffinose** and **stachyose**.

- **Raffinose** is composed of galactose, glucose, and fructose.  Found in beans, cabbage, brussels sprouts, broccoli and whole grains
- **Stachyose** is made up of two galactose, a glucose, and a fructose.  Its found in beans and other legumes

Because we don't have the enzymes to break these down, they go into the large intestine undigested.

### Polysaccharides
These guys contain hundreds to thousands of glucose molecules.  They include **starch**, **glycogen**, and **fibers**.

#### Starch
Plants store glucose not as single molecules but as polysaccharides in the form of starch.  The two forms are **amylose** and **amylopectin**.  **Amylose** is a straight chain of glucose molecules while **amylopectin** is branched a bunch.  They are both in starch foods.  Amylopectin is more rapidly digested and raises blood glucose faster than amylose.  Grains and legumes and tubers are where these are found.  They use alpha bonds usually, and are easily digestible, but the ones that are not are called resistant.  Legumes are these ones.

#### Glycogen
Glycogen is the way that glucose gets stored in all animals.  When an animal gets killed most of its glycogen gets broken down by enzymes in their tissues.  This causes not a lot of glycogen to exist in meat.  Since plants don't have any in them, its not considered a dietary source of carbohydrate.  We store glycogen in our muscles and liver.  It is very easily broken down into glucose in our liver when we need it for energy.

#### Fiber
Fiber is the polysaccharide that contains beta bonds.  They go straight through our digestive track without being digested and absorbed and therefore contribute no energy to our diet.  But they have lots of other health benefits.

- **Dietary fiber** is the non-digestable parts of plants that form the main support structures of leaves, stems, and seeds.  They're like a plants skeleton
- **Functional fiber** comes from sus laboratory things and includes cellulose, guar gum, pectin, inulin, and psyllium
- **Total fiber** is the sum of the two
