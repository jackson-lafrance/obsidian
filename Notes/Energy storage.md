## How is energy stored
Stored energy is needed during times of sleep, fasting or exercise when energy demands persist but food isn't being consumed.  The body stores extra energy as fat as [[Triglycerides|triglycerides]] or carbohydrates as [[Complex carbohydrates|glycogen]].  We can store unlimited fat but only a certain amount of glycogen.  We cannot store amino acids or nitrogen, so most of them get bound up in [[Protein basics|protein]] molecules.  This makes triglycerides the most useful form of energy since they are the most abundant.

### Dietary glucose is stored as muscle and liver glycogen
Small amounts of carbs are stored as glycogen in the body in the livers and muscles.  The amount you store can vary anywhere from 200g to 1200g.  Amount in muscles is 2->3x more than that in the liver.

### The energy of dietary triglycerides is stored as adipose tissue
When we eat in excess the body uses carbs for energy and stores fat as body fat.  This is because of:
- The conversion of dietary fat to body fat is easy and doesn't really need any energy
- Dietary fatty acids can be used by adipose tissue cells and converted into stored triglycerides without changing the fatty acids original structure
- Converting dietary carbs to fatty acids needs a lot of steps and is energy inefficient
- When carbs are consumed in excess, there is an increase in the oxidation of glucose over fat for energy which leaves more fat available for storage

### The energy of dietary proteins is circulating amino acids
Even though the body has not storage spot for extra protein, some free amino acids can be quickly broken and used for energy as necessary.
