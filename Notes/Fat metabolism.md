## Glycerol is converted to pyruvate
The small three carbon backbone of [[Triglycerides|triglycerides]] (glycerol) doesn't make a lot of energy but is important for other metabolic functions.  The liver can easily turn it into pyruvate for the Krebs cycle or it can be used to regenerate glucose.  So when we have low carbs we can use glycerol as a source of glucose.

## Fatty acids are converted to acetyl CoA
Fatty acids released from [[Lipid digestion|adipose cells]] attach to **albumin**, a blood protein and go to cells that need energy where they are catabolized for energy through a process called fatty acid oxidation.  This happens in the [[Mitochondria and ATP|mitochondria]].  To get there they needed to be activated by CoA which requires an "investment" amount of ATP.  The activated guys then go across the membrane thanks to a compound called **carnitine**.  

A 16-carbon fatty acid is then turned into 8 acetyl CoA guys thanks to oxidation.  Similar to [[Carbohydrate metabolism|glucose]].

## Fatty acids cannot turn into glucose
Glycerol can turn into glucose with the middle man pyruvate but fatty acids cannot turn into glucose.

## Ketones are a by-product of fat catabolism
The acetyl CoA can come from glucose or fatty acid catabolism.  Without enough carbs to do the TCA cycle we convert the extra acetyl CoA to **ketone bodies**.  These are released into the bloodstream by the liver where they can be used as an alternative fuel.  The production of energy from ketones is metabolically inefficient since the ATP produced is less than if they were used by B-oxidation of fatty acids.  It is more like a backup than a viable strat.
