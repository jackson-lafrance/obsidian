## Maintaining energy homeostasis
To maintain homeostasis we have to regulate [[Energy storage|energy storage]] and breakdown.  A bunch of hormones do this by signaling cells to either store nutrients or mobilize them for energy.  The balance between these anabolic and catabolic hormones determines whether the body is in a building or breaking state at any given time.

## Insulin is the primary anabolic hormone
**Insulin** is produced by the beta cells of the pancreas and increases in the blood after a meal, especially one rich in [[Carbohydrate basics|carbohydrates]].  It is the primary [[Metabolism overview|anabolic]] hormone.  It activates enzymes that promote the storage of nutrients and lets cells take up glucose from the bloodstream.

Once inside the cells, these nutrients are made into [[Complex carbohydrates|glycogen]], [[Triglycerides|triglycerides]], and body [[Protein basics|protein]].  Insulin effectively turns off catabolic processes within the body so that nutrients are directed towards storage and tissue building rather than breakdown.

Insulin also promotes lipogenesis in the liver and inhibits [[Fat metabolism|lipolysis]] in adipose tissue, meaning it both encourages fat creation and discourages fat breakdown.  This is why chronically elevated insulin levels (from constant snacking or high sugar diets) make it harder to lose body fat.

**Fructose** is unique among sugars because it doesn't directly stimulate insulin release.  It is metabolized almost entirely by the liver rather than being taken up by cells throughout the body the way glucose is.  This is part of why high fructose consumption can contribute to fatty liver disease and elevated blood lipids without triggering the same satiety signals that glucose does.

## Catabolic hormones trigger energy mobilization
**Glucagon**, **epinephrine**, and **cortisol** are the main catabolic hormones.  They turn off anabolic processes and prompt the body to break down stored triglycerides, glycogen, and body proteins for energy.

### Glucagon
Produced by the alpha cells of the pancreas, glucagon is released when blood glucose drops (between meals or during fasting).  It signals the liver to break down glycogen into glucose (**glycogenolysis**) and to produce new glucose from noncarbohydrate sources (**[[Biosynthesis pathways|gluconeogenesis]]**).  Glucagon and insulin work as opposing forces to keep blood glucose within a normal range.

### Epinephrine
Also known as adrenaline, epinephrine is released from the adrenal glands during the fight-or-flight response (stress, exercise, danger).  It rapidly mobilizes energy by stimulating glycogen breakdown in both the liver and muscles and by triggering the release of fatty acids from adipose tissue.  Its effects are faster but shorter-lived than glucagon's since the goal is immediate energy availability.

### Cortisol
Cortisol is a steroid hormone released from the adrenal cortex during prolonged stress.  It promotes the breakdown of muscle protein into [[Amino acids and peptides|amino acids]] that can be used for gluconeogenesis, and it stimulates lipolysis in adipose tissue.  Chronically elevated cortisol (from long-term stress, lack of sleep, or overtraining) promotes fat storage especially around the abdomen and accelerates muscle protein loss.
