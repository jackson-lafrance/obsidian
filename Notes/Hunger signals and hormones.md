## The role of nerve cells
The nerve cells lining the [[Stomach|stomach]] and small [[Intestines|intestine]] detect changes in pressure according to whether their respective organs are empty or distended with food.  The cells relay this data to the [[Appetite and hunger|hypothalamus]], which makes us hungry.

Nerve cells in the [[Mouth|mouth]], [[Pharynx and esophagus|pharynx]], and esophagus get triggered by chewing and swallowing food and then let the hypothalamus know, which makes us full.

## The role of hormones
Hormones are molecules, either [[Protein basics|proteins]], or [[Lipid basics|lipids]], that get secreted into the bloodstream by one of the **endocrine glands** of the body.  In the bloodstream they are like chemical messengers, which bind to and trigger a response in target cells far away from where they were produced.  Hormones important for hungry and satiety are as follows.

**Insulin and glucagon** are hormones produced in the pancreas and are responsible for maintaining blood glucose levels.  Glucose, a [[Simple carbohydrates|simple sugar]], is the body's most readily available fuel supply.  This is why its level in our blood is an important signal in regulating hunger.  When we haven't eaten for a while, our blood glucose is low, which decreases the level of insulin and increases the level of glucagon.  This message gets relayed to the hypothalamus and prompts us to eat.  After we eat, the sensation of distention in the stomach and small intestine, and the rise in blood glucose as we absorb our food.  This triggers an increase in insulin secretion and decrease in glucagon, and the feeling of fullness.

**Ghrelin** is made by our stomach, and right after we eat it plummets.  As time increases ghrelin follows, which quickly triggers the hypothalamus to make us eat.  Opposite to this is **cholecystokinin** (CCK), which is made by the small intestine when food enters it, it stimulates the satiety centre.

**Leptin** is a protein produced by fat cells that tells the hypothalamus to suppress hunger and increase metabolism (doesn't work on obese people).  When we eat more than we burn, the extra nutrients get converted to fat and stored in [[Lipid digestion|adipose tissue]] which then produces more leptin.

## The role of amount and type of food
Foods with protein have the highest satiety value, because they increase the secretion of satiety hormones and reduce that of ghrelin.  Fat is higher than carbs aswell.  Another factor that affects hunger is how much fibre and water are in the food, since these stretch the stomach and small intestine, which sends signals to the hypothalamus that we are full and tells us to stop eating.
