## How do feeding and fasting affect metabolism
Although we always need energy, some people fuel their body on an intermittent basis.  Feeding is [[Metabolism overview|anabolic]] and fasting is catabolic.  So if we fast for a short amount of time we can shift to a catabolic profile which will force the body to break down [[Fat metabolism|fatty acids]] as fuel since we will run out of glucose.  The longer you are fasted the greater the rate of [[Biosynthesis pathways|gluconeogenesis]].

During the first ~12 hours of fasting, the body relies on liver [[Complex carbohydrates|glycogen]] to maintain blood glucose.  Once glycogen stores are depleted, the body increasingly turns to fatty acid oxidation and gluconeogenesis from [[Amino acids and peptides|amino acids]] and glycerol.  After extended fasting (~24+ hours), [[Fat metabolism|ketone body]] production ramps up to provide an alternative fuel source for the brain and other tissues.

Prolonged starvation is dangerous though.  Eventually the body has no energy reserves left and cannot maintain blood glucose levels, which leads to organ failure.  Muscle wasting accelerates as the body catabolizes its own [[Protein basics|protein]] to keep gluconeogenesis going.

## Time-Restricted Eating (TRE)
TRE limits the daily eating window while keeping total caloric intake roughly the same.  The idea is that extending the overnight fasting period shifts the body into a more catabolic state for longer, promoting fat oxidation and giving digestive organs a rest.

- **16:8** — 16 hours fasting, 8 hour eating window (most popular)
- **14:10** — 14 hours fasting, 10 hour eating window (more moderate)
- **12:12** — 12 hours fasting, 12 hour eating window (minimal restriction, close to normal)
- **Early TRE (eTRE)** — Eating window in the morning/afternoon (e.g. 7am-3pm).  Aligns food intake with circadian rhythm since [[Hunger signals and hormones|insulin]] sensitivity and metabolic rate are higher earlier in the day.  Studies suggest eTRE may have greater benefits for blood glucose regulation than late TRE.
- **Late TRE** — Eating window in the afternoon/evening (e.g. 12pm-8pm).  More socially practical since dinner is included, but less aligned with circadian biology.

## Intermittent Calorie Restriction (ICR)
ICR alternates between normal eating days and reduced calorie or full fasting days rather than restricting the daily eating window.

- **ADF (Alternate Day Fasting)** — Alternating between a normal eating day and a fasting day (zero or very low calories ~500).  Most aggressive form of ICR.
- **4:3 diet** — Eat normally for 4 days, fast or heavily restrict for 3 days per week
- **5:2 diet** — Eat normally for 5 days, restrict to ~500-600 calories on 2 non-consecutive days per week.  Most sustainable of the ICR approaches.

The key difference between TRE and ICR is that TRE controls *when* you eat each day while ICR controls *how much* you eat on specific days.  Both can shift the body towards a more catabolic, fat-burning profile, but the mechanisms and sustainability differ per person.
