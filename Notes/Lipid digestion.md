## How does the body process lipids?
Since lipids aren't soluble in water they can't easily enter the bloodstream from the GI tract.  This makes their digestion, absorption, and transport different than that of carbs and proteins.

**Lingual lipase** is a salivary enzyme that plays a small role in the breakdown of lipids, but most of them reach the stomach intact.  In the stomach they get mixed around and broken up into smaller droplets.

### The gallbladder, liver, and pancreas assist in fat digestion
Since lipids aren't soluble in water, their digestion needs bile from the gallbladder and digestive enzymes from the pancreas.  Both of these organs are attached to the small intestine and as lipids enter the small intestine the gallbladder contracts and sends out bile.  This is caused by the release of **cholecystokinin** from the duodenal mucosal cells into the circulation.  **Secretin**, another hormone released by these duodenal mucosal cells also helps the gallbladder contract.  The same guys also cause the pancreatic aqueous phase and digestive enzymes to get released into the gut.

Even though the gallbladder stores bile, its actually produced in the liver.  **[[Phospholipids and sterols|Lecithins]]** are the primary emulsifier in the bile, because their hydrophobic tails attract lipid droplets and cluster them together.  They let the bile act like soap which breaks up the lipids into smaller and smaller droplets that have a bigger surface area.

## Absorption of lipids occurs primarily in the small intestine
The mucosal lining of the small intestine is where most lipid absorption occurs with the help of the **micelles**.  Micelles are spherical compounds made up of bile salts and biliary phospholipids that can capture the lipid digestion products and take them to the [[Nutrient absorption and transport|enterocytes]] for absorption.  The micelle has a hydrophobic core and a hydrophilic surface, which is great for transporting lipids in the gut.

How do the absorbed lipids that don't mix with water get into the bloodstream?  They get turned back into [[Triglycerides|triglycerides]] in the enterocytes and packaged into lipoproteins.  **Lipoproteins** are spherical compounds with triglycerides in the centre along with cholesterol esters, free cholesterols, and other hydrophobic guys, with phospholipids and proteins making the outside of the sphere.  The main lipoprotein made in the enterocytes to transport lipids from a meal is called **chylomicron**.

**LPL** releases triglycerides from the chylomicrons so they can be used throughout the body in cells.  When chylomicrons touch a cell, they come into contact with lipoprotein lipase which breaks apart the triglycerides at their core and frees up the individual fatty acids to move into the cell.

The remnant chylomicrons are now just filled with cholesterol, phospholipid, and protein and are removed from the blood by the liver which recycles them.

Short and medium chained fatty acids are easier to transport since they don't have to be reformed into triglycerides and made into chylomicrons.  Instead they can go straight into the portal bloodstream bound to either the transport protein **albumin** or a phospholipid.

### Fat is stored in adipose tissues for later use
The three primary fates for fatty acids are:
1. Body cells (especially muscle cells) can take them and use them as a source of energy
2. Cells can use them to make lipid-containing compounds needed by the body
3. If the body doesn't need them right away, muscle and adipose cells can re-create the triglycerides and store them for later use

The main storage site for triglycerides is the **adipose cell**.  Its the only body cell that has enough storage capacity for them.  If you're more active though, your body will store them in muscle tissues instead.  This makes sure that the next time you go on a run the fat will be ready to be used as energy.  This is why more physically active people have less body fat and more muscle mass.
