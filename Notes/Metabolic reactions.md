## Metabolic pathways
Metabolic pathways are clusters of chemical reactions that occur in a row to achieve a goal, like the breakdown of glucose for energy.  Cells use different pathways to release the energy in each of the major energy containing nutrients.  These happen in different parts of the cell.

The [[Mitochondria and ATP|mitochondria]] is like the furnace in your house, it has a lot of metabolic enzymes and is the primary site where ATP is produced.  Metabolic pathways are limited to specific cell types, structure, and specific body organs/tissues.  Glycogen stored in the liver can be catabolized and released into the bloodstream but glycogen in skeletal muscle cannot be.  This is due to muscle lacking the enzyme that catalyzes glycogen.

## Dehydration synthesis and hydrolysis
These are chemical reactions involving water.  The first one is also known as condensation and is an anabolic process.  This is when small guys mix together to make a big molecule and water gets produced as a by product.  This formula is:

A - OH + H - B -> A - B + H2O

An example of this is making [[Simple carbohydrates|disaccharides]] from monosaccharides.  Hydrolysis is the opposite catabolic process, which is when water is added to a complex molecule to break it apart.  It happens like 

A - B + H2O -> A - OH + H - B

## Phosphorylation
This process is when one more more phosphate groups are added to a chemical compound.  Glucose undergoes it when it enters a cell

Glucose + ATP -> Glucose-6-phosphate + ADP

The newly phosphorylated glucose can either be stored as glycogen or used for immediate energy.  This is also used to make ATP from ADP + free phosphate.  The removal/breakdown of ATP is called dephosphorylation.

## Oxidation and reduction reactions
These are exchange reactions where one donates a hydrogen and the other accepts it.  The molecule that donates is oxidized and the molecule that accepts is reduced since it gains an electron.  This produces energy through a series of oxidation reduction reactions that end up making CO2 and H2O.  Like the oxidation of glucose and of fatty acids.

## Enzymes mediate metabolic reactions
During energy metabolism, one function of enzymes is to move energy-containing nutrients into their respective metabolic pathways.  The body can increase or decrease the activity of a particular enzyme to channel fatty acids toward a breakdown for energy or towards being stored as adipose tissue.  This makes enzymes important to regulate metabolism.

In order to work, enzymes need **cofactors**, small, nonprotein guys that enhance or are needed for the action of the enzyme.  Cofactors help bind different parts of an enzyme together and speed up the reaction.  If a cofactor is organic it is called a **coenzyme** (organic if it has carbon).
