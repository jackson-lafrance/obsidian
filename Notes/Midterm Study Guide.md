# 1. Information Literacy (5 Q)

**Scientific method steps:** Observation → Question → Hypothesis → Experiment → Analysis → Conclusion

**Types of studies (weakest → strongest):**
- **Case report** — one person, no control
- **Observational (epidemiological)** — watches groups, finds correlations, **CANNOT establish causation**
- **Cross-sectional** — snapshot in time
- **Cohort** — follows groups over time
- **Case-control** — compares sick vs healthy, looks backward
- **Randomized controlled trial (RCT)** — gold standard, random assignment, CAN show causation
- **Meta-analysis / systematic review** — combines multiple studies, strongest evidence

**Key point:** Observational studies show correlation NOT causation. Only RCTs can establish cause and effect.

**Types of publications:**
- **Peer-reviewed journal articles** — most reliable
- **Textbooks** — good summaries, can be outdated
- **Websites/news** — least reliable, check the source

**Dietary reference intakes (DRIs):**
- **EAR** (Estimated Average Requirement) — meets needs of 50% of population
- **RDA** (Recommended Dietary Allowance) — meets needs of 97-98% of population
- **AI** (Adequate Intake) — used when not enough data for RDA
- **UL** (Tolerable Upper Intake Level) — max safe daily amount
- **AMDR** (Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Range) — % of calories from carbs/fat/protein

**CER framework:** Claim → Evidence → Reasoning

# 2. Biochemical Molecules (6 Q)

## The four macromolecule families

| Molecule | Building blocks | Key bonds | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carbohydrates** | Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose) | Alpha/beta glycosidic | Energy (4 kcal/g) |
| **Lipids** | Fatty acids + glycerol | Ester bonds | Energy storage (9 kcal/g), insulation, membranes |
| **Proteins** | Amino acids (20 total) | Peptide bonds | Structure, enzymes, transport, immunity (4 kcal/g) |
| **Nucleic acids** | Nucleotides | Phosphodiester bonds | Genetic info (DNA/RNA) |

## Carbs
- **Mono:** glucose (most abundant), fructose (sweetest), galactose, ribose (in RNA)
- **Di:** sucrose (glu+fru), lactose (glu+gal, beta bond), maltose (glu+glu)
- **Poly:** starch (plant storage, alpha bonds), glycogen (animal storage), fiber (beta bonds, indigestible)
- Alpha bonds = digestible, Beta bonds = indigestible (except by bacteria)

## Lipids
- **Triglycerides** (95% of dietary fat) = glycerol + 3 fatty acids
- Saturated = no double bonds, solid, bad (butter, lard)
- Monounsaturated = 1 double bond (olive oil)
- Polyunsaturated = 2+ double bonds (omega-3, omega-6)
- **Trans fats** = hydrogenated, worst for health
- **Phospholipids** = hydrophobic tail + hydrophilic head → cell membranes
- **Sterols** = ring structure → cholesterol

## Proteins
- 20 amino acids, 9 are **essential** (must eat them)
- Structure: primary → secondary → tertiary → quaternary
- **Denaturation** = loses shape and function (heat, acid, alcohol)
- **Complete proteins** = all 9 essential AAs (animal sources, quinoa)

## Nucleic acids
- **DNA** = double strand, A-T, G-C, deoxyribose
- **RNA** = single strand, A-U, G-C, ribose
- Store and transfer genetic info for protein synthesis

## Where is energy produced?
- **Cytosol** = glycolysis
- **Mitochondria** = Krebs cycle + electron transport chain (95% of ATP)

# 3. Digestion and Absorption (7 Q)

## GI tract order
**Mouth → Pharynx → Esophagus → Stomach → Small intestine → Large intestine → Rectum**

## Organ-by-organ

**Mouth:**
- Mechanical: chewing (mastication)
- Chemical: **salivary amylase** (starts starch/carb digestion), **lingual lipase** (starts lipid digestion)

**Esophagus:**
- **Peristalsis** moves bolus to stomach
- **Epiglottis** covers trachea during swallowing

**Stomach:**
- **HCl** denatures proteins, activates pepsinogen → **pepsin**
- **Pepsin** digests proteins
- **Gastric lipase** does a little lipid digestion
- **Gastrin** hormone controls HCl and pepsin release
- Food becomes **chyme** (liquid)
- **Pyloric sphincter** controls release to small intestine

**Small intestine** (most digestion + absorption):
- **Duodenum** → **Jejunum** → **Ileum**
- **Pancreatic amylase** finishes carb digestion
- **Proteases** (trypsin, chymotrypsin, carboxypeptidase) finish protein digestion
- **Bile** (from liver, stored in gallbladder) emulsifies fats
- **Pancreatic lipase** finishes lipid digestion
- **Brush border enzymes:** maltase, sucrase, lactase break disaccharides → monosaccharides

**Large intestine:**
- Absorbs water, electrolytes, vitamins
- **GI flora** (probiotics) ferment fiber
- No real digestion, stores waste 12-24 hours

## Four types of absorption (in small intestine)
1. **Passive diffusion** — no energy, no carrier, follows concentration
2. **Facilitated diffusion** — no energy, uses carrier protein (fructose)
3. **Active transport** — needs ATP + carrier protein, against gradient (glucose, amino acids)
4. **Endocytosis** — cell membrane engulfs particles (antibodies from breast milk)

## Key enzyme-function matches (EXAM FAVORITE)
| Enzyme | Digests | Where |
|---|---|---|
| Salivary amylase | Starch → maltose | Mouth |
| Lingual lipase | Lipids | Mouth |
| Pepsin | Proteins | Stomach |
| Gastric lipase | Lipids | Stomach |
| Pancreatic amylase | Starch → maltose | Small intestine |
| Trypsin/chymotrypsin | Proteins → peptides | Small intestine |
| Pancreatic lipase | Lipids → FA + glycerol | Small intestine |
| Maltase/sucrase/lactase | Disaccharides → mono | Small intestine (brush border) |

## Transport after absorption
- **Water-soluble** (glucose, amino acids) → blood capillaries → portal vein → **liver**
- **Fat-soluble** (lipids) → packaged into **chylomicrons** → **lacteals** (lymph) → bloodstream

# 4. Catabolic Metabolism (7 Q)

## The big picture
Glucose/fatty acids/amino acids → Acetyl CoA → Krebs → ETC → **ATP**

## Stage 1: Glycolysis (cytosol, NO oxygen needed)
- Glucose → 2 pyruvate
- Costs 2 ATP, makes 4 ATP = **net 2 ATP**
- Also makes 2 NADH
- First step is phosphorylation: glucose + ATP → glucose-6-phosphate

## What happens to pyruvate?

**WITH oxygen (aerobic):**
- Pyruvate → **Acetyl CoA** (in mitochondria)
- Releases CO2, makes NADH
- **Irreversible** — acetyl CoA CANNOT become glucose again
- Then enters Krebs cycle

**WITHOUT oxygen (anaerobic):**
- Pyruvate → **lactate**
- No extra ATP, but regenerates NAD+ so glycolysis can continue
- Happens in muscles during intense exercise
- Lactate goes to liver → remade into pyruvate → glucose

## Stage 2: Krebs Cycle (TCA) (mitochondrial matrix)
- Acetyl CoA enters the cycle
- Per glucose: produces 6 CO2, 2 GTP (=2 ATP), 8 NADH, 2 FADH2
- Coenzymes (NADH, FADH2) carry electrons to Stage 3

## Stage 3: Electron Transport Chain (inner mitochondrial membrane)
- NADH and FADH2 donate electrons
- Electrons pass down chain of carrier proteins
- Energy used to make ATP (oxidative phosphorylation)
- O2 is the final electron acceptor → becomes H2O
- **~30 net ATP per glucose total** (all three stages combined)

## Fat catabolism
- **Lipolysis**: triglycerides → glycerol + fatty acids
- **Glycerol** → can become pyruvate → glucose (small amount of energy)
- **Fatty acids** → activated by CoA → enter mitochondria via **carnitine**
- **Beta-oxidation**: fatty acid chain chopped into 2-carbon acetyl CoA units
- Acetyl CoA → Krebs → ETC → lots of ATP
- **Fatty acids CANNOT become glucose** (one-way street)
- Excess acetyl CoA (low carb) → **ketone bodies** (backup fuel, less efficient)

## Correct order for lipid mobilization in fasted state:
**Lipolysis → Beta-oxidation → Acetyl-CoA formation → Ketone synthesis**

## Protein catabolism
- **Proteolysis**: proteins → amino acids
- **Deamination**: removes NH2 group → leaves carbon skeleton (keto acid)
- NH2 → converted to **urea** (excreted by kidneys)
- Carbon skeleton → enters glycolysis or Krebs for energy
- **Glucogenic** amino acids → can make glucose
- **Ketogenic** amino acids → can make ketones/fatty acids

## Correct order for amino acid catabolism:
**Transamination → Deamination → Urea formation → Entry into Krebs cycle**

## Key intersections to know
- **Acetyl CoA** is the crossroads — glucose, fatty acids, and amino acids all funnel into it
- **Pyruvate** is where carbs and some amino acids meet
- **Oxaloacetate** keeps Krebs running — needs carb-derived molecules

# 5. Anabolic Metabolism (7 Q)

## Energy storage
- **Glycogen** = stored glucose (liver + muscle, limited capacity 200-1200g)
- **Adipose tissue** = stored triglycerides (unlimited capacity)
- **No storage form for amino acids** — excess protein → fat

Why fat is stored preferentially:
- Converting dietary fat → body fat is easy and cheap (energy-wise)
- Converting carbs → fat is expensive (many steps)
- When carbs are abundant, body burns glucose and stores fat

## Gluconeogenesis (making NEW glucose)
- Happens in the **liver**
- From **non-carbohydrate** sources: glucogenic amino acids, glycerol, lactate
- NOT from fatty acids (they can't become glucose!)
- Used during fasting, sleep, exercise to maintain blood glucose
- Rate increases the longer you fast

## Lipogenesis (making NEW fat)
- Also called **de novo synthesis**
- Happens in **liver cells**
- From excess carbs, ketogenic amino acids, alcohol
- Many steps, energy-expensive

## Cholesterol synthesis
- Made from **acetyl CoA** in liver and intestine
- Body makes MORE than we eat
- Used for: bile acids, steroid hormones, vitamin D, cell membranes

## Transamination
- How we make **nonessential amino acids**
- Carbon skeleton from carbs + amine group donated from another amino acid
- Glucogenic AAs → can make glucose
- Ketogenic AAs → can make ketones/fatty acids
- Some AAs are both

# 6. Intermittent Fasting (5 Q)

## Hormones in fed vs fasted state

### FED STATE (anabolic)
- **Insulin** ↑↑↑ (primary anabolic hormone)
  - Lets cells take up glucose
  - Promotes glycogen synthesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis
  - Turns OFF catabolic processes
  - Released from beta cells of pancreas after meals (especially carbs)
  - **Fructose does NOT trigger insulin** (metabolized only by liver)

### FASTED STATE (catabolic)
- **Glucagon** ↑ — signals liver to break down glycogen + do gluconeogenesis
- **Epinephrine** ↑ — rapid energy mobilization (fight or flight), glycogen breakdown + lipolysis
- **Cortisol** ↑ — breaks down muscle protein for gluconeogenesis, promotes abdominal fat storage when chronic

### Key: insulin and glucagon are opposing forces that keep blood glucose stable

## Fed-fast cycle
- **Fed state:** insulin dominant, nutrients stored (glycogenesis, lipogenesis)
- **Early fasting (0-12h):** glucagon rises, liver glycogen → glucose
- **Extended fasting (12-24h):** glycogen depleted, gluconeogenesis ↑, fatty acid oxidation ↑
- **Prolonged fasting (24h+):** ketone production ↑, brain uses ketones as backup fuel
- **Goal of the entire cycle = maintain normal blood glucose**
- **Brain consumes energy throughout** (it never stops)
- Ketones preserve functional proteins (so body doesn't eat all its muscle)
- **FALSE that it's mainly regulated by cortisol** — it's mainly insulin/glucagon

## Time-Restricted Eating (TRE)
| Pattern | Fasting | Eating |
|---|---|---|
| 16:8 | 16h | 8h |
| 14:10 | 14h | 10h |
| 12:12 | 12h | 12h |

- **Early TRE (eTRE)** = eat morning/afternoon → better for blood glucose (matches circadian rhythm)
- **Late TRE** = eat afternoon/evening → more practical socially

## Intermittent Calorie Restriction (ICR)
- **ADF** = alternate day fasting (eat/fast/eat/fast)
- **4:3** = eat 4 days, fast 3 days
- **5:2** = eat 5 days, restrict to ~500 cal on 2 days

TRE = controls **when** you eat. ICR = controls **how much** on certain days.
