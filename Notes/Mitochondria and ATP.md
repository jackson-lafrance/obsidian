## Mitochondria structure
- A mitochondrion has a double membrane (unusual)
- Outer membrane surrounds the organelle
- Inner membrane contains numerous folds that are called **cristae**
- The **matrix** is the fluid that is enclosed by the cristae
- Cristae increase the membrane surface area in contact with the matrix, and allow for more attached protein complexes and enzymes involved in making ATP from ADP and P
- Metabolic enzymes in the matrix are used to catalyze reactions that release carbon dioxide and provide extra energy for cell's functions
- Mitochondria can vary widely in shape, they can be long and slender, short and fat, but they all share the basic structural features I've explained
- The number of mitochondria in a given cell changes depending on the cells needs for energy
	- For example, 30% of the volume of a heart muscle cell is mitochondria but there are none in red blood cells
- Mitochondria have their own DNA and ribosomes.  The **mtDNA** codes for small numbers of RNA and polypeptide molecules.  The polypeptides are used in enzymes required for energy production
- Although mitochondria have their own genetic system to make proteins, their functions depend on their imported proteins coded by nuclear DNA

## ATP production
1. Although most ATP production occurs in the mitochondria, it starts in **cytosol**.  This is called **glycolysis**, where each glucose molecule is split into two molecules of **pyruvate**.  The mitochondria then absorbs the molecules.

2. In the matrix of the mitochondria, a CO2 is removed from each absorbed pyruvate molecule.  The rest enters the **citric acid cycle** which breaks down the absorbed pyruvate remnant into CO2 and H atoms.  The CO2 diffuses into the cytoplasm.

3. The H atoms are delivered to enzymes of the cristae, which catalyze **ATP synthesis** from ADP and P.  At the end of this process, O and H combine to form H2O.

4. The energy released during these steps supports the conversion of ADP to ATP which leaves the mitochondrion.

The amount of ATP generated during glycolysis is tiny compared to the amount made by the breakdown of pyruvate in the mitochondria.  Because mitochondrial activity requires oxygen, its called **cellular respiration**.  This produces 95% of the ATP needed to keep a cell alive.  Mitochondria are like little cellular batteries that provide the energy needed to power cellular functions.
