Some nutrient absorption happens in the stomach and large intestine but most of it happens in the small intestine.  Its large surface area and special absorptive cells are essential to this task.

## Specialized lining
The lining of the small intestine is especially well made for absorption.  If you were to examine the mucosa of the small intestine, you would notice it is heavily folded.  This increases its surface area which allows it to absorb more nutrients than if it were flush.

Within these folds there are small, fingerlike, projections called villi, whose constant movement helps them find and trap nutrients.  They are made up of a bunch of specialized absorptive cells called **enterocytes**.  Inside each one are capillaries and a **lacteal**, which is a small lymphatic vessel.  The capillaries absorb the water soluble nutrients ([[Protein basics|proteins]] and [[Carbohydrate basics|carbs]]) into the bloodstream while the lacteal absorbs fat-soluble nutrients ([[Lipid basics|lipids]]) into the lymph.  Each enterocyte has hairlike guys on it called microvilli.  They look like tiny toothbrushes and are called the **brush border** collectively.  They increase the surface area of the small intestine like crazy, increasing its absorptive capacity as well.

## Four types of absorption occur in the small intestine
Nutrients get absorbed across the mucosal membrane and into the bloodstream or lymph through 4 transport processes.
1. Passive diffusion is the process by which nutrients cross into the enterocytes without the use of anything else.  It can occur when the wall of the intestine is permeable to the nutrient and the concentration of the nutrient in lumen is higher than its concentration in the enterocytes.  Thus, the nutrient is just naturally moving
2. Facilitated diffusion occurs when nutrients get moved across the enterocytes with the help of a carrier protein.  They don't need any energy still and they are driven by concentration.  Fructose is transported like this.
3. Active transport needs energy (ATP) in combination with a carrier protein.  The energy and the protein allow for the nutrients to be absorbed against their concentration gradient, which means they can go from low to high concentration areas.  Glucose, amino acids, and some minerals are absorbed this way.
4. Endocytosis is a method of active transport where a small amount of the intestinal contents are engulfed by the enterocyte's membrane and taken into the cell.  Some stuff like breast milk's antibodies and other large particles are absorbed this way.

## Blood and lymph transport nutrients and wastes
Two circulating fluids are used to transport nutrients and waste products throughout the body.  

Blood goes through the cardiovascular system and lymph goes through the lymphatic system.  Oxygen we inhale in our lungs is carried by our red blood cells which goes to the heart and then the rest of the body.  Blood goes to all of our tissues to deliver oxygen, nutrients, and other materials.  As well as to pick up carbon dioxide and other waste products.  

In the enterocytes, blood in the capillaries picks up water and water-soluble nutrients while lymph in the lacteals picks up most lipids and fat-soluble vitamins.  Lymph nodes are clusters of immune cells that filter microbes and other bad guys from the lymph fluid.  Eventually it all goes back to the bloodstream at an area near the heart where they join together.

When blood leaves the GI tract, it gets transported by the portal vein to the liver.  The waste products picked up by the blood are filtered out and excreted by the kidneys.  Also, most carbon dioxide left in the blood is exhaled once it reaches the lunchs, which makes room for oxygen to attach to the red blood cells once more.
