## The esophagus propels food into the stomach
The mass of food that is chewed and moistened in the [[Mouth|mouth]] is called a bolus.  This gets swallowed and propelled into the [[Stomach|stomach]] through the esophagus.  When swallowing, a tiny flap covering the entrance to the windpipe (**trachea**) called the **epiglottis** acts like a trapdoor covering the entrance.  It is normally open so that we can breathe but when the food approaches our brain is like yo block that bih.  But sometimes this doesn't work.

As the trachea closes, at the same time, the sphincter muscle at the top of the esophagus opens up to let food come in.  The esophagus then transports the food to the stomach.  Its squeezes and propels food along its length by contracting the circular and longitudinal muscles.  This is called peristalsis.

Gravity also helps with this, which is why its important to sit or stand upright while you are eating.  Combined, the two can transport a bit of food in 5 seconds.  At the end of the esophagus is another sphincter (**gastroesophageal sphincter**/**lower esophageal sphincter**), which is usually tightly locked.  When food reaches the end, the sphincter relaxes and lets some food come in.  Some people have weird sphincters that are always react and this is a disorder.
