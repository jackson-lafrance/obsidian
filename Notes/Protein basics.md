## What are proteins
Proteins are large and complex molecules found in every living thing.  Proteins are important parts of all tissues of the human body including bones, blood, skin, and of course muscle.  They also function in metabolism, immunity, fluid balance, and nutrient transport, and they can provide energy sometimes.

## The building blocks of proteins are amino acids
Proteins are made of CHO but also a special form of N that the body can use readily.  Carbs and lipids don't provide N.  Proteins are available from lots of both plant and animal foods.  Also we can synthesize them thanks to instructions given by our DNA.

Proteins in our body are made from a combination of amino acids.  These are molecules composed of a central carbon atom connected to four other groups:
- **Amine group**
- **Acid group**
- **H atom**
- **Side chain**

The first three always stay the same but the side chain is what makes each amino acid unique.  Most of the body's proteins are made from combinations of just 20 amino acids.  We have 10->50k unique proteins that can be made from just 20 amino acids in various sequences.

The difference between proteins and stuff like carbs is that while a carb like starch is a chain of glucose molecules, the protein insulin is made up of 51 [[Amino acids and peptides|amino acids]] connected in specific order.

## Protein turnover involves synthesis and degradation
Our bodies constantly need new proteins to work properly.  Protein turnover is when new proteins are made and old proteins are degraded to make the building blocks for these new proteins.  This process lets the cells respond to the constantly changing demands of the body.

## Protein organization determines function
Four levels of protein structure exist:
1. The primary structure of the protein is the order of the amino acids
2. The secondary structure is the twisting/folding of the amino acid chain
3. The tertiary structure is another folder than makes the protein 3D
4. In proteins with quaternary structure, two or more polypeptides interact making a larger protein (Used in stuff like muscle contraction)

The importance of the shape of a protein to its functions is massive.  For example, the protein strands in muscle fibres are longer than they are wide so that they can contract and relax.  In contrast, those in red blood cells are globular in shape so red blood cells are like flattened discs with depressed centres.  This structure lets them change shape and flow freely through even the smallest capillaries while retaining their original shape.

## Protein denaturation affects shape and function
Proteins can uncoil and lose their shape when they are exposed to heat, acids, bases, heavy metals, alcohols, and other damaging substances.  This is called **denaturation**.  This is stuff like the stiffening of egg whites when they are whipped, the curdling of milk when lemon juice is added, or the solidifying of eggs as they cook.  

Denaturation doesn't affect the primary structure of proteins, but its function is lost.  When critical body proteins are exposed to head or acid it stops them from performing their functions which can happen during times of high fever or when blood pH is off.  Sometimes this is helpful (digestion).

## Protein synthesis can be limited by missing amino acids
All essential amino acids must be available for protein synthesis to occur, if not the missing one is called the **limiting amino acid**.  Without the proper combination, protein synthesis slows to a point where they can't even be generated.  If hemoglobin contains the essential amino acid histidine for example, if we don't consume enough of it, we become unable to produce hemoglobin.

Another thing that inhibits protein synthesis is inadequate energy consumption (not eating enough calories!!).  If we don't have enough energy in our diets, our bodies won't be able to use any amino acids for energy and this will stop them from producing proteins.

Proteins taht don't contain all of the essential amino acids in sufficient quantities too support growth and health are called **incomplete proteins**.  Proteins with all nine of the essential amino acids are called **complete proteins**.  Most of these are from animal sources but quinoa also works.  Also taking multiple incomplete proteins together can create a complete protein source.
