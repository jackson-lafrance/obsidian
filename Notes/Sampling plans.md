There are two fundamental ways to collect data:

- Experiment
	- Where the researcher **imposes** conditions or treatments on experimental units to generate data
- Observational study
	- The data already exists and the researcher just measures the variable of interest

## Sampling plan types
The method used to get a sample from the population of interest is called a sampling plan

The sampling plan should incorporate **randomization** to reduce bias.

### Simple random samples
This is the most common type of sampling plan.

If a sample of $n$ guys is selected from a population of $N$ guys,
Each guy has the same chance to get selected,
Then it is random and the resulting sample is a *simple random sample*

To select a random sample, the following steps are used:
1. Identify the population of interest
2. Obtain the **sampling frame**, list of all the units
3. Assign a number to each one
4. Generate random numbers to identify which units to include until the desired sample size is reached

### Stratified random sample
This one you divide the population into sub-populations/**strata** and selected a simple random sample from each strata.

### Cluster sample
Divide the population into subgroups called clusters, select a random sample of clusters and take a **census** of *every element in the cluster* 

### 1 - in - k systematic sample
Randomly select one of the first $k^{th}$ elements in an ordered set and then select every $k^{th}$ element thereafter

### Non random samples
**\*The guys below are bad since they aren't random**
- **Convenience sample**: a sample that can be taken easily without random selection, like people walking by on the street
- **Judgement sample**: the sampler decides who will and won't be included in the sample
- **Quota sample**: the makeup of the sample reflects the makeup of the population on some selected characteristic (race, ethnic origin, gender, etc)

## Sampling biases
- **Non response bias**: Only 30% of a mailed survey are returned. However, over 90% of the surveys returned agree with a proposed zoning change.
- **Under-coverage bias**: Researcher conducts survey by calling only landline phone numbers, by interviewing people at a shopping mall on a weekday afternoon, studying exercise habits using data from a fitness app.
- **Wording bias**: "How often do you eat *unhealthy* fast food", "Do you support the government's *costly* tax increase", "Don't you think stricter speed limits would make our roads *safer*"
