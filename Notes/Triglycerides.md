## Triglycerides are the most common form of lipid
Triglycerides are 95% of the fat that we eat, and they are in the same form in which most body fat is stored.  A triglyceride is a molecule consisting of a **3 carbon glycerol** backbone and a **3 fatty acid chain**, with **one** attached to each carbon.

Triglycerides form when three fatty acids attach to glycerol through **dehydration synthesis** reactions.  They're also known as **condensation reactions**.

## Why are some triglycerides better than others
They vary by their chain length and level of saturation and shape, which can depend on how they're processed.  All of these factors determine how triglyceride is used within our body and how it affects our health.

### Chain length affects digestion and absorption
The fatty acids attached to the backbone can vary in number of carbons, they usually contain 4 -> 24 with the most common amount being 18.

- **Short chain fatty acids** are usually less than 6
- **Medium chain fatty acids** are 6 -> 12
- **Long chain fatty acids** are 14 or more

The carbons can be numbered starting with the carbon of the COOH (**carboxyl**) end, which is called the **alpha** carbon.  The ending CH3 (**methyl**) group's carbon is called the **omega**.

The length of the chain determines the method of digestion and absorption by our body and affects how they get metabolized.  Short and medium chains are quickly digested since they are needed for energy.  Large amounts of them though cause GI distress and have a negative impact on blood lipids.  Long-chains are the most abundant.

### Hydrogen saturation influences health effects
They can also vary by the type of carbon bonds they contain.  In a fatty acid most carbons are bounded to two other carbons and two hydrogens, but sometimes they can have double bonds.  The less hydrogen, the less saturated.

- **Saturated fatty acid** - no double bonds
	- Commonly found in coconut oil, butter, cheese, whole milk, cream, lard, and beef fat
- **Monounsaturated fatty acid** - one double bond
	- Usually liquid at room temp and include things like olive oil, canola oil, peanut oil, and cashew nuts
- **Polyunsaturated fatty acid** - more double bonds
	- Depending on the carbon it is next to, they are either **omega-3** or **omega-6** fatty acids.  Also liquid at room temp and include soy, canola, corn, and safflower oils

Plants are more unsaturated while animals are more saturated.

### Carbon bonding influences shape
Saturated fatty acids are easier to stack and can pack very tightly since they have no kink.  This is why they are solid at room temp.  The double bond of unsaturated fats though gives them a kink and that makes them liquid since they can't pack as tightly.

### Trans fatty acids are really bad
Unsaturated fatty acids are either **cis** or **trans** shaped.  Cis shaped ones have a kink since they have both hydrogens in each double bond on the same side.  These are in plant oils, veggies, and whole grains.  Trans fatty acids have hydrogen atoms attached on both sides.  Trans fatty acids are mostly commercially produced by breaking the double bond of a cis one and inserting an extra hydrogen on each side.

This happens when oils are **hydrogenated** which straightens them out and makes them solid at room temp.  This helps foods like cakes, cookies, and crackers resist rancidity, since the extra hydrogen stops the carbon atoms from oxidizing.

Hydrogenation can be controlled to make more or less of the oil saturated.  If only some double bonds are broken, the fat produced is **partially hydrogenated**.

The straight rigid shape of saturated and trans fats we eat have bad effects on our health.  They raise blood cholesterol levels and change cell membrane function + the way cholesterol gets removed from our blood.  Diets high in them increase risk for CVD.

### Essential fatty acids have unique health benefits
- **Omega-6** acids, linoleic acid is found mostly in vegetables and nut oils.  It gets metabolized to **arachidonic acid** which is the precursor to eicosanoids
- **Omega-3** acids, alpha-linolenic acid protects against CVD.  Only a bit of ALA is converted into EPA and DHA, so we have to consume those directly through fish
